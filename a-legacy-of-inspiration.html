A Legacy of Inspiration

(Mason)

Solid Foundation

Working at BP gave many young women a solid foundation from which they ventured on their intellectual journeys. Seeing the effects that their efforts had on the world inspired both Betty Webb and Mavis Batey, who worked on Japanese translation and German codebreaking. This was the case for many young women. Even though they were not allowed to speak about their work because of the Official Secrets Act, the confidence gained from achieving the impossible cannot be understated.
(Howard, Kerry, Bletchley Park Research)
"My time at Bletchley Park gave me greater confidence to decide my own future. I don't recall ever having a plan for my future before the war...My time at Bletchley Park changed my life. Writing my book about that time has enriched my life further."
- Betty Webb, ATS
"...working for an eccentric genius whose motto was 'nothing is impossible' at the most formative years of my life clearly made a lasting impression....He [Dilly Knox] always impressed on us that a scholar must see work through from beginning to end, whatever that entails."
- Statement of Mavis Batey, read by former BBC Chief New Correspondent, Kate Adle
(Royal United Services Institute for Defense and Security Studies)

Inspiration of Women in Cybersecurity &  Beyond

(Horne)
"...Bletchley Park was a prime example of how women can and did take leading roles in the security workplace."
- Dr. Brooke Hoskins, Director of Program Management, Government Relations & Strategy, Raytheons Women Network 
The Women of BP to this day act as inspiration to women in the cybersecurity fields. CyberSecurityChallengeUK hosts an annual event where they invite veteran women from BP as well as women working in cybersecurity today. These modern women find endless inspiration from those who came before.
"Unfortunately, as they were sworn to secrecy – the contribution is only known by those who look into the history as you are doing. Women inherently tend to get on with the job at hand without shouting from rooftops, how, why or what they are doing. We need more role-models. This is changing."
- Stephanie Aldridge, in a personal interview
"We have learned from the Bletchley Park women that we [as women] can and do make a difference."
- Jean Macintyre, in a personal interview
"Today we encourage young and old, women and men, to train for cyber security. Cyber is exciting, knowing that you are doing your bit to protect your country...... just like the Bletchley Park girls (and boys) it was an amazing project and we will always be eternally grateful for their efforts."
- Jean Macintyre, in a personal interview
"I came here thinking that the people that worked at Bletchley Park were probably 50-year-old blokes sitting around in tweed jackets smoking pipes and doing the Times Crossword. That was my idea of a codebreaker. But when I came here and found out that, in fact, more than ten thousand people worked here and that more than half of them were women, and that the work done here is said to have shortened the war by about two years. At that time, about eleven million people a year were dying, so twenty-two million lives [were] potentially saved by the work done here. And that work wasn't done by 50-year-old blokes. Those ten thousand people were mainly young people from sixteen upwards, and some of the older people were in their thirties. So amazing: a place full of teenagers, people in their twenties - ten thousand of them working here, saving millions of lives."
- Dr. Sue Black, computer science professor, ran the "Saving Bletchley Park" campaign

Contributions to the Arts

"The program, the Bletchley Circle] starts in 1943, introducing the characters during World War II. All too soon, nine years have passed and the body of the story is set in the early 1950's.  War has ended and the women have assimilated into their peacetime lives but continue to meet. They notice patterns in crimes that the police have missed. The women decide to follow the clues and solve the crime."
- Kerry Howard, Bletchley Park Researcher & Historian
The ingenuity of the women working at BP resonated so strongly with the creators of "The Bletchley Circle" that they chose to display their stunning intellectual abilities through fiction. 
"The Bletchley Circle ... finds an imaginative way to give overdue credit to those unrecognized government servants, most of whom were women. "
- Alessandra Stanley, The New York Times
"I wanted to see these women’s brains working; I wanted to dramatize their brains, [and] not just them giggling and having fun. "
- Sarah Harding, director of "The Bletchley Circle," in a personal interview
