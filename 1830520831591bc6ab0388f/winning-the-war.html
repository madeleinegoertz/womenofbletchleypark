Winning the War

(Mason)

Shortened the War

Cracking the Enigma allowed the Allies to read German messages, letting them position the troops into strategic locations which saved many lives. Sir Harry Hinsley has estimated that millions of innocent lives were saved due to this. Inadvertently, BP kept many families whole by decoding the Enigma.
(Eisenhower)
"My own conclusion is that it shortened the war by not less that two years and probably by four years - that is the war in the Atlantic, the Mediterranean and Europe."
- Sir Harry Hinsley, Temporary Senior Administrative Office and official historian of British Intelligence in World War II (Hinsley, Sir Harry 9-10)
"Every one of those ships before it was attacked and sunk had to be sighted by a British aeroplane or submarine which had been put in a position in which it would sight it without it knowing that it had been put in that position, and had made a sighting signal which the Germans and the Italians had intercepted. That was the standard procedure. As a consequence of that the Germans and the Italians assumed that we had 400 submarines whereas we had 25. And they assumed that we had a huge reconnaissance airforce on Malta, whereas we had three aeroplanes!"
- Sir Harry Hinsley

Avoiding U-Boats

"In World War II,  Hitler commissioned an encrypting machine that would be unbreakable. The Lorenz was used to encrypt teleprinter messages. Lorenz SZ40s were first used in June 1941, and the SZ42 from mid-1942 onwards. The SZ42 had 12 wheels with different numbers of cams or pins that could produce 1.6 billion different combinations. The Allies did not see a Lorenz machine until after the war but dubbed the machine and its messages "Tunny". Enigma, an entirely different system, was used for operational messages and was much less complex."
- The National Museum of Computing 
German U-boats sank supply ships destined for the United Kingdom which could have starved the nation into submission. However, cracking the Enigma allowed the supply ships to arrive safely in Britain while letting Allied airplanes sink U-boats as well. The work done at BP not only shortened World War II, but it also saved the supply ships and therefore, the people of the British Isles.
("German U-Boat")
"The only thing that ever really frightened me during the war was the U-boat peril."
- Sir Winston Churchill
"It has been calculated that the Ultra saved about one and [a] half million tons [of cargo] in September, October, November and December '41."
- Sir Harry Hinsley 

Colossus for Lorenz

When the Lorenz Enigma was introduced, it was clear the Bombe was not sufficient for the task. Drawing upon the Bombe's concepts, Tommy Flowers designed a new machine called Colossus. It decoded Lorenz messages, but perhaps more importantly, it sparked a flame called computers.
(Crypto)
"In addition, those men and women, recruited mainly from universities, developed methods and machinery of a sophistication hitherto undreamt of, including as you all know the first operational electronic computer which was called Colossus."
- Sir Harry Hinsley
(Colossus)
"By the end of the war ten Colossus machines were working around the clock to find the start wheels of the Lorenz-encrypted messages. They made a huge impact by accelerating the code-breaking and enabling many more messages to be deciphered. It is sometimes said that the Allies may have been able to read some messages even before Hitler's generals had seen them."
- The National Museum of Computing
