Golden Geese

(Mason)

Joan Clarke

Joan Clarke reached the highest tier in the cryptanalysis department while decrypting the Naval Enigma. Clarke inspired women by working in a male-dominated department and applying her analytical thinking and mathematics skills to the war effort. This led to continued progress on the decryption of the Naval Enigma and the acceptance of women in intellectual positions at BP.
(Sykes)
"[Banburimus were] so enthralling that the analyst due to go home at the end of the shift would be unwilling to hand over the workings......I had been a particular culprit, being in a billet within cycling distance instead of having to catch official transport."
- Joan Clarke, leading Bletchley Park cryptographer
 “Welcome to the sahibs’ room.”
- Kendrick, leading Bletchley Park cryptographer
"The story of WWII and technological advance, in so many ways, is the story of women doing math."
- Margot Lee Shetterly, author of Hidden Figures

Margaret Rock & Mavis Lever

Working with Dillwyn "Dilly" Knox, Margaret Rock and Mavis Lever were supreme codebreakers who pioneered a method to crack the Abwehr Enigma. They showed that women can thrive if only given a chance.
"[Margaret Rock] is entirely in the wrong grade. She is actually 4th or 5th best in the whole Enigma staff and quite as useful as some of the 'profesors'. I recommend that she should be put on to the highest possible salary for anyone of her seniority."
- Dillwyn "Dilly" Knox, leading Bletchley Park cryptographer
(Horne)
Rock (Simkin)
Dillwyn Knox “attributes the success to two young girl members of his staff, Miss Rock and Miss Lever, and he gives them all the credit.”
- Alastair Denniston, first head of the Government Code & Cipher School (GC&CS)
Lever (Crowley)
"[Mavis Lever is] the most capable and the most useful."
- Dillwyn "Dilly" Knox

Betty Webb

While Betty Webb paraphrased decrypted Japanese messages, she also gained knowledge and confidence which allowed her to succeed later in life. Currently, Betty tells her story to people around the world and inspires them with her key role in the in the codebreaking effort. 
(Webb, Charlotte, S/Sgt Betty Vine-Stevens)
"I only knew, as we all did, what was going on in the actual room in which we were working because we were not allowed to talk about our work outside our own officers. It was tight security; security was extremely tight."
- Betty Webb, in a personal interview
"I did a lot of things actually, bearing in mind I was very young and very inexperienced generally, and I wasn’t trained for anything in particular but I was given lots of fairly junior jobs to do particularly registering all the messages that came in from signal stations around the world. That was one of my main jobs. And then my most important job was actually paraphrasing the decoded Japanese messages. That was towards the end of my time there."
- Betty Webb, in a personal interview
"My job was just to paraphrase, in other words to turn them into different wording which meant the same thing but which would not be recognized by the enemy had they picked them up...well that was what we hoped anyway."
- Betty Webb, in a personal interview
"I started off as a lance-corporal and ended up as a staff sergeant, staff sergeant, which is when I went to Washington I was a staff sergeant."
- Betty Webb, in a personal interview

Mary Thompson

"I’m very proud to say that my mother Mary, as  she preferred to be called, worked at Bletchley Park from 1943 to the end of the war as a Morse slip reader, ending up as a sergeant in charge of a watch at the tender age of 20. She left school at 15, never went to university but, in those incredibly formative years found fulfillment in a demanding and important job and made lasting friendships. But of course none of us at home knew that when I was growing up. Like all the others who signed the official secrets act here, my mum kept mum. She told no one about the nature of her work – not parents , husband or children."
- Sarah Harding, daughter of DM Thompson, director of "The Bletchley Circle" 
"We sat at covered typewriter keyboards on tables surrounding the large room with a thin paper slip running through a metal channel at eye level. The slip showed Morse in the form of a positive wave as per an oscilloscope. High for dash and slightly shorter for dots. Most of us were not conversant with the typewriter keyboard and had been trained in Morse sound via headphones. It was like starting from scratch."
​- Mary Thompson

​- Sarah Harding
