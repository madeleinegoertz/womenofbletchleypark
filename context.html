An Introduction to Bletchley Park

(Draco2008)
(Bletchley Park Reunion)
BP housed the headquarters of British codebreaking operations during WWII. The German enciphering method used for communication, the Enigma, was decrypted here. Germans used the Enigma to send indecipherable messages regarding bombing raids and U-boat attacks.
(Manske)
"The women employed at Bletchley Park (commonly referred to as 'girls') were both civilians and servicewomen, generally young and often well-educated, not seldom to university level...they were primarily found in supporting positions, such as clerical tasks and machine-minding. [A] few women can be found higher up in the organization, or in more prestigious [departments, such as] intelligence work and cryptanalysis."
​- Annie Burman, author of "Gender Decryption - Decrypting Gender​"

The Women's Land Army

(Women's Land Army)
During the war, “women’s work” was often repetitive and required little skill. If a woman joined the WLA, she spent her time bailing hay, plowing fields, and tending crops. These women were invaluable, but if a woman wanted to use her intellectual prowess to further the cause, she was out of luck.
(Women's Land Army 115_2725030)
"I remember going to one place with another girl. The Lady of the Manor took us to a field about one-and-a-half acres. It was covered with weeds and thistles almost as tall as ourselves. We were told to clear it. We had no gloves to wear, so you can imagine what our hands were like at the end of the day. Even our faces were scratched. She used to sit in her car at the far end of the field to watch us. If it rained and we went to shelter under a tree, she would come round and make us go back."
- Grace Wallace, member of the Women's Land Army

Bletchley Park as an Intellectual Outlet

(Burman 27)
The counter-intelligence work at Bletchley posed the perfect challenge for these women. Here they could hold positions which utilized their unique abilities. It acted as an outlet for women to use both their intellects and serve their country at the same time.
"...[My job was] both demanding and interesting." I was "contented, busy, efficient, and convinced that the work I did was really useful. "
- Elizabeth Greaves, ATS
"Some [women] claim they refused to take days off because they were so fascinated."
- Ann Harding, wireless operator
"I think it was a great education because it was a very mixed bag of people from all walks of life but mainly very intelligent people. And it was a great eye-opener for me and a really good education ... it was just mixing with the people who were there and integrating with them. I think it educated me probably in a better way than had I simply gone to school or  university."
- Betty Webb, ATS, in a personal interview
